* Part I — Divisibility, associates, primes, irreducibles
** NEXT Define divisibility in an integral domain
   - a ∣ b iff ∃c, b = ac
   - unit
   - associate elements
   - associate iff differ by multiplication by a unit

** NEXT Define irreducible
   For nonzero nonunit p:
   - p irreducible iff p = ab implies a unit or b unit

** NEXT Define prime element
   For nonzero nonunit p:
   - p prime iff p ∣ ab implies p ∣ a or p ∣ b

** NEXT Prove prime implies irreducible in any integral domain

** NEXT Prove irreducible implies prime in a UFD
   This is needed for using “prime” and “irreducible” interchangeably in UFDs.

** NEXT Normalize “up to associates”
   Add lemmas:
   - associated irreducibles divide each other
   - if irreducible p divides irreducible q, then p and q are associates
   - divisibility is invariant under associates

* Part II — Unique Factorization Domains
** NEXT Define finite factorization
   Represent a factorization of nonzero a as:
   - unit u
   - finite multiset/list of irreducibles
   - a = u * product factors

** NEXT Define UFD
   Requirements:
   - every nonzero nonunit has a finite factorization into irreducibles
   - factorization unique up to permutation and associates

** NEXT Prime-exponent form
   Given a finite set of pairwise nonassociate primes p_1,...,p_n:
   - define exponent vector
   - define u * Π p_i^{e_i}

** NEXT Divisibility criterion in a UFD
   If
   - a = u Π p_i^{e_i}
   - b = v Π p_i^{f_i}
   then
   - a ∣ b iff e_i ≤ f_i for all i, after a common prime support is chosen.

** NEXT Prime occurrence lemmas
   If q divides a product Π p_i^{e_i}, with q irreducible/prime, then q is associate to some p_i with e_i > 0.

* Part III — GCDs in a UFD
** NEXT Define greatest common divisor in an integral domain
   d is gcd(a,b) iff:
   - d ∣ a
   - d ∣ b
   - for every c, c ∣ a and c ∣ b implies c ∣ d

** NEXT Prove gcd is unique up to associates

** NEXT Prove UFD gcd formula
   Given:
   - a = u Π p_i^{e_i}
   - b = v Π p_i^{f_i}
   with common support p_i,
   define:
   - d = Π p_i^{min(e_i,f_i)}
   Prove:
   - d is a gcd of a and b

** NEXT Handle edge cases
   - gcd(a,0)
   - gcd(0,b)
   - gcd of units
   - gcd formula when all exponents are zero gives 1

** NEXT Example

* Part IV — LCMs in a UFD

** NEXT Prove lcm is unique up to associates

** NEXT Prove UFD lcm formula

* Part V — PIDs imply UFDs
** NEXT Define PID
   Integral domain where every ideal is principal.

** NEXT Ascending chain lemma for PIDs
   Prove every ascending chain of ideals stabilizes:
   - I_1 ⊆ I_2 ⊆ ...
   - union I is an ideal
   - I = (a)
   - a ∈ I_n for some n
   - hence I = I_n and chain stabilizes

** NEXT Existence of irreducible factorization in a PID
   For nonzero nonunit r:
   - if r irreducible, done
   - otherwise split r = r_1 r_2 with both nonunits
   - strict ideal inclusion (r) ⊂ (r_1)
   - infinite splitting would yield forbidden ascending chain
   - conclude termination

** NEXT Uniqueness of factorization in a PID
   Use:
   - irreducible implies prime in PID
   - or prove via principal prime ideals
   Then induct on number of irreducible factors.

** NEXT Theorem
   - every PID is a UFD

** NEXT Corollary
   - every Euclidean domain is a UFD

** NEXT Fundamental theorem of arithmetic
   Instantiate:
   - Z is Euclidean
   - hence Z is a UFD

* Part VI — Dedekind-Hasse norms
** NEXT Define Dedekind-Hasse norm
   A norm N : R -> Nat satisfying the relevant Dedekind-Hasse condition:
   for a,b nonzero, if a ∉ (b), then ∃ nonzero r ∈ (a,b) with N(r) < N(b).

** NEXT Multiplicative Dedekind-Hasse norm from PID
   For PID/UFD:
   - N(0)=0
   - N(unit)=1
   - N(a)=2^n where n is number of irreducible factors of a
   Prove:
   - well defined by uniqueness of factorization
   - positive
   - multiplicative
   - Dedekind-Hasse property

** NEXT Equivalence target
   Combine with previous result:
   - integral domain with Dedekind-Hasse norm is PID
   - PID has multiplicative Dedekind-Hasse norm

* Part VII — Gaussian integers Z[i]
** NEXT Define Gaussian integers
   As pairs (a,b) : Z × Z representing a + bi.
   Operations:
   - addition componentwise
   - multiplication (a,b)(c,d) = (ac - bd, ad + bc)
   - conjugation (a,b)^* = (a,-b)
   - norm N(a+bi)=a^2+b^2

** NEXT Ring structure
   Prove:
   - commutative ring with 1
   - integral domain
   - conjugation is ring automorphism
   - N(αβ)=N(α)N(β)

** NEXT Units in Z[i]
   Prove:
   - α unit iff N(α)=1
   - units are ±1, ±i

** NEXT Euclidean domain structure
   Formalize if not already:
   - Z[i] is Euclidean with norm a^2+b^2
   - hence PID
   - hence UFD

** NEXT Irreducibility by prime norm
   General quadratic-integer lemma:
   - if |N(α)| is a rational prime, then α is irreducible

** NEXT Prime elements of Z[i]

* Part VIII — Fermat theorem on sums of two squares
** NEXT Squares modulo 4
   Prove:
   - every integer square is 0 or 1 mod 4
   - if odd prime p = a^2+b^2, then p ≡ 1 mod 4

** NEXT Lemma: p divides n^2+1 iff p=2 or p≡1 mod 4
   For odd prime p:
   - p ∣ n^2+1 iff -1 has square root mod p
   - iff (Z/pZ)^× has element of order 4
   - iff 4 ∣ p-1
   Needed lemmas:
   - unique element of order 2 in (Z/pZ)^× is -1
   - if 4 ∣ |G| for finite abelian G with unique element of order 2, then G has element of order 4
   Alternative later route:
   - use cyclicity of (Z/pZ)^× once available

** NEXT If p≡1 mod 4, p reducible in Z[i]
   Choose n with p ∣ n^2+1.
   Since p ∣ (n+i)(n-i), if p were prime in Z[i], p would divide n+i or n-i.
   Derive contradiction because then p divides 2i.

** NEXT Fermat theorem
   Prove:
   - rational prime p is a sum of two integer squares iff p=2 or p≡1 mod 4
   - uniqueness up to signs and swapping

** NEXT Factorization of primes in Z[i]
