* Rudiments of Several Complex Variables
** Section 1: Cauchy's Formula and Applications

*** Core notation
**** Ambient space
- Work in \(\mathbf{C}^n\)
- Write
  - \(z = (z_1,\dots,z_n)\)
  - \(z_j = x_j + \sqrt{-1}\, y_j\)

**** Norm and inner-expression notation
- Define
  - \(w = (z,z) = \sum_{i=1}^n |z_i|^2\)

**** Function spaces
- For \(U \subseteq \mathbf{C}^n\) open:
  - \(C^\infty(U)\): smooth functions on \(U\)
  - \(C^\infty(\overline{U})\): functions smooth in a neighborhood of \(\overline{U}\)

*** Differential-geometric setup
**** Real cotangent basis
- Basis:
  - \(dx_j\)
  - \(dy_j\)

**** Complex cotangent basis
- Use instead:
  - \(dz_j = dx_j + \sqrt{-1}\,dy_j\)
  - \(d\overline{z}_j = dx_j - \sqrt{-1}\,dy_j\)

**** Dual tangent basis
- Corresponding vector fields:
  - \(\frac{\partial}{\partial z_j}\)
  - \(\frac{\partial}{\partial \overline{z}_j}\)

**** Total differential
- Express \(df\) in the complex basis
- Track decomposition into \(dz_j\) and \(d\overline{z}_j\) parts

*** One complex variable: basic definitions
**** Holomorphic function
- A smooth function \(f\) on \(U \subseteq \mathbf{C}\) is holomorphic iff
  - \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}} = 0\)

**** Cauchy-Riemann equations
- Write
  - \(f(z)=u(z)+\sqrt{-1}\,v(z)\)
- Then holomorphicity is equivalent to the Cauchy-Riemann equations

**** Analytic function
- \(f\) is analytic at \(z_0\) iff it has a local power series expansion
  - \(f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (z-z_0)^n\)
- Convergence is absolute and uniform on some disc around \(z_0\)

**** First major theorem
- \(f\) is holomorphic iff \(f\) is analytic

*** Cauchy Integral Formula
**** Statement
- For \(A\) a disc in \(\mathbf{C}\), \(f \in C^\infty(A)\), and \(z \in A\):
  - express \(f(z)\) as a boundary integral
  - plus an area term involving \(\partial f/\partial \overline{w}\)

**** Orientation
- Boundary line integrals are taken counterclockwise

**** Proof strategy
- Use Stokes' theorem on a differential form with a singularity
- This method is later formalized in Chapter 3

**** Singular differential form
- Introduce the form
  - \(\eta = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\frac{f(w)\,dw}{w-z}\)

**** Key computation
- For \(z \neq w\), compute \(d\eta\)
- Obtain an expression involving
  - \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{w}}\)
  - \(dw \wedge d\overline{w}\)

**** Punctured disc argument
- Remove a small disc \(A_\varepsilon = A(z,\varepsilon)\)
- Apply Stokes on \(A - A_\varepsilon\)

**** Boundary decomposition
- Boundary consists of:
  - outer boundary \(\partial A\)
  - inner boundary \(\partial A_\varepsilon\)

**** Limit of the small-circle term
- Parameterize \(w-z = re^{i\theta}\)
- Show the inner boundary integral tends to \(f(z)\) as \(\varepsilon \to 0\)

**** Integrability of the area term
- Use
  - \(dw \wedge d\overline{w} = -2i\,dx \wedge dy\)
- Show the area integrand is absolutely integrable

**** Conclusion
- The formula follows by passing to the limit \(\varepsilon \to 0\)

*** Proposition: holomorphic iff analytic
**** Statement
- For \(U \subseteq \mathbf{C}\) open and \(f \in C^\infty(U)\):
  - \(f\) holomorphic iff \(f\) analytic

**** Proof direction 1: holomorphic implies analytic
- Assume
  - \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}} = 0\)
- Apply Cauchy’s formula on a small disc around \(z_0\)
- Expand
  - \(\frac{1}{w-z}\)
  as a geometric series around \(z_0\)
- Define coefficients
  - \(a_n\) by boundary integrals
- Conclude
  - \(f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (z-z_0)^n\)
- Convergence is absolute and uniform on smaller discs

**** Proof direction 2: analytic implies holomorphic
- Assume \(f\) has a convergent power series expansion
- Partial sums satisfy Cauchy’s formula without the area term
- Pass to the limit using uniform convergence
- Differentiate under the integral sign
- Conclude
  - \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}} = 0\)

*** Final one-variable result
**** Goal
- Given \(g \in C^\infty(A)\) on a disc \(A\), solve
  - \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}} = g\)
  on a slightly smaller disc

**** Name
- \(\overline{\partial}\)-Poincaré Lemma in one variable

**** Solution formula
- Define
  - \(f(z)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_A \frac{g(w)\,dw \wedge d\overline{w}}{w-z}\)

**** Claims
- \(f\) is well-defined
- \(f\) is smooth in \(A\)
- \(f\) satisfies
  - \(\frac{\partial f}{\partial \overline{z}} = g\)

** Weierstrass Preparation Theorem

*** Setup
- \(f(z_1,\dots,z_{n-1}, w)\)
- \(f(0)=0\)
- Nontrivial dependence on \(w\)

*** Assumption
- Power series contains:
  - term \(a w^d\), \(a \neq 0\)

*** Key idea
- Look at roots \(b_i(z)\) of:
  - \(f(z,w)=0\)

*** Construct symmetric functions
- Power sums:
  - \(s_k(z)\)
- Elementary symmetric polynomials:
  - \(\sigma_i(z)\)

*** Build polynomial
- Define:
  - \(g(z,w) = w^d + a_1(z)w^{d-1} + \cdots + a_d(z)\)

*** Properties
- \(g\) vanishes on same zero set as \(f\)

*** Define quotient
- \(h = f/g\)

*** Key step
- Show:
  - removable singularities ⇒ extend holomorphically

*** Final statement
- \(f = g \cdot h\)

**** Properties
- \(g\): Weierstrass polynomial
- \(h(0) \neq 0\)

*** Uniqueness
- Coefficients determined by integral formulas

* Rudiments of Several Complex Variables — Part III
** Analytic Varieties

*** Definition: analytic variety
- Let \(U \subset \mathbf{C}^n\) be open.
- A subset \(V \subset U\) is an analytic variety if for every \(p \in U\), there exists a neighborhood \(U'\) of \(p\) such that
  - \(V \cap U'\) is the common zero locus of finitely many holomorphic functions
  - \(f_1,\dots,f_k\) on \(U'\)

*** Definition: analytic hypersurface
- \(V\) is an analytic hypersurface if locally it is the zero locus of a single nonzero holomorphic function \(f\)

*** Definition: irreducible
- \(V\) is irreducible if it cannot be written as
  - \(V = V_1 \cup V_2\)
  - with \(V_1,V_2\) analytic varieties and neither equal to \(V\)

*** Definition: irreducible at a point
- \(V\) is irreducible at \(p \in V\) if for sufficiently small neighborhoods \(U'\) of \(p\),
  - \(V \cap U'\) is irreducible

---

** Irreducibility of hypersurfaces from irreducible germs

*** Statement
- If \(f \in \mathcal{O}_n\) is irreducible, then the analytic hypersurface
  - \(V = \{f=0\}\)
  is irreducible at \(0\)

*** Proof idea
- Assume \(V = V_1 \cup V_2\)
- Choose \(f_1,f_2 \in \mathcal{O}_n\) vanishing on \(V_1,V_2\) respectively
- By the Nullstellensatz:
  - \(f\) divides \(f_1f_2\)
- Since \(f\) is irreducible:
  - \(f \mid f_1\) or \(f \mid f_2\)
- Contradiction

---

** Local decomposition of analytic hypersurfaces

*** Statement
- If \(V \subset U \subset \mathbf{C}^n\) is an analytic hypersurface, then near any point \(p \in V\),
  - \(V\) can be written uniquely as a finite union
  - \(V = V_1 \cup \cdots \cup V_k\)
  - where each \(V_i\) is irreducible at \(p\)

*** Mechanism
- Use unique factorization in \(\mathcal{O}_n\):
  - \(f = f_1 \cdots f_k\)
  - with each \(f_i\) irreducible
- Then
  - \(V_i = \{f_i = 0\}\)

---

** Varieties cut out by two functions

*** Setup
- Let \(W \subset U \subset \mathbf{C}^n\) be defined near \(0\) by
  - \(f = 0\)
  - \(g = 0\)

*** Assumption
- \(W\) contains no analytic hypersurface through \(0\)

*** Consequence
- \(f\) and \(g\) are relatively prime in \(\mathcal{O}_n\)

*** Coordinate choice
- After linear combinations, assume:
  - neither \(\{f=0\}\) nor \(\{g=0\}\) contains the line \(\{z' = 0\}\)
- Then:
  - \(f\) and \(g\) may be taken to be Weierstrass polynomials in \(z_n\)

*** Resultant
- Define the resultant
  - \(\gamma = \alpha f + \beta g \neq 0\)
  - with \(\gamma \in \mathcal{O}_{n-1}\)

*** Projection statement
- Let
  - \(\pi: \mathbf{C}^n \to \mathbf{C}^{n-1}\)
- Then the image \(\pi(W)\) is the zero locus of \(\gamma\)

*** Geometric interpretation
- Locally, projection expresses \(W\) as a finite-sheeted branched cover over an analytic hypersurface in \(\mathbf{C}^{n-1}\)

*** Further projection
- After projection onto a suitable \((n-2)\)-plane:
  - \(W\) becomes locally a finite-sheeted branched cover of a neighborhood in \(\mathbf{C}^{n-2}\)

---

** Hypersurface criterion from projection

*** Setup
- Let \(V \subset U \subset \mathbf{C}^n\) be irreducible at \(0\)
- Assume that for arbitrarily small neighborhoods \(A\) of \(0\),
  - \(\pi(V \cap A)\) contains a neighborhood of \(0\) in \(\mathbf{C}^{n-1}\)

*** Local defining equations
- Write
  - \(V = \{f_1 = \cdots = f_k = 0\}\)

*** Key claim
- The \(f_i\) must have a common factor in \(\mathcal{O}_n\)

*** Reason
- Otherwise \(V\) would lie in the common zero set of two relatively prime functions
- By the previous projection result:
  - \(\pi(V \cap A)\) would be a proper analytic subvariety of \(\mathbf{C}^{n-1}\)
- Contradiction

*** Conclusion
- Let \(g\) be the gcd of the \(f_i\)
- Then irreducibility forces
  - \(V = \{g=0\}\)
- So \(V\) is an analytic hypersurface near \(0\)

---

** General local picture for analytic varieties

*** Stated without proof
- These analogues hold for general analytic varieties:

**** Decomposition into irreducible components
- Near any point \(p\),
  - \(V\) can be uniquely written as
  - \(V = V_1 \cup \cdots \cup V_r\)
  - where each \(V_i\) is irreducible at \(p\)

**** Projection theorem
- Any analytic variety can be expressed locally by projection as a finite-sheeted cover of a polydisc, branched over an analytic hypersurface

**** Image under projection
- If \(V \subset \mathbf{C}^n\) does not contain the line
  - \(z_1 = \cdots = z_{n-1} = 0\)
- Then the projection of a neighborhood of \(0\) in \(V\) to \(\mathbf{C}^{n-1}\) is an analytic subvariety near \(0\)

*** Technical note
- The main difficulty is showing that an arbitrary zero locus in a polydisc is defined by finitely many holomorphic functions in a slightly smaller polydisc

*** Forward reference
- These facts follow from the proper mapping theorem

** Complex Manifolds

*** Definition: complex manifold
- A complex manifold \(M\) is a differentiable manifold with an open cover \(\{U_\alpha\}\) and coordinate maps
  - \(\varphi_\alpha : U_\alpha \to \mathbf{C}^n\)
- Such that transition maps
  - \(\varphi_\alpha \circ \varphi_\beta^{-1}\)
  are holomorphic wherever defined

*** Holomorphic functions on a manifold
- A function on \(U \subset M\) is holomorphic if in every chart it becomes a holomorphic function on an open subset of \(\mathbf{C}^n\)

*** Holomorphic coordinates
- A tuple \(z=(z_1,\dots,z_n)\) on \(U\subset M\) is a holomorphic coordinate system if it gives holomorphic changes of coordinates with the atlas

*** Holomorphic maps
- A map \(f:M\to N\) of complex manifolds is holomorphic if in local holomorphic coordinates it is given by holomorphic functions

---

** Examples of complex manifolds

*** Riemann surfaces
- A one-dimensional complex manifold is a Riemann surface

*** Projective space
- \(\mathbf{P}^n\) is the set of lines through the origin in \(\mathbf{C}^{n+1}\)

**** Homogeneous coordinates
- Write points as
  - \([Z_0,\dots,Z_n]\)

**** Standard affine charts
- On \(U_i = \{Z_i \neq 0\}\), define coordinates by dividing by \(Z_i\)

**** Structure
- Transition maps are holomorphic
- Therefore \(\mathbf{P}^n\) is a complex manifold

**** Important facts
- \(\mathbf{P}^n\) is compact
- \(\mathbf{P}^1\) is the Riemann sphere
- Linear subspaces of \(\mathbf{P}^n\) come from linear subspaces of \(\mathbf{C}^{n+1}\)
- Hyperplanes in \(\mathbf{P}^n\) are parametrized by the dual projective space \((\mathbf{P}^n)^\vee\)

**** Affine compactification picture
- \(\mathbf{C}^n\) sits inside \(\mathbf{P}^n\)
- The complement is the hyperplane at infinity

*** Complex tori
- If \(\Lambda \subset \mathbf{C}^n\) is a discrete lattice, then
  - \(\mathbf{C}^n / \Lambda\)
  inherits a complex manifold structure
- It is compact iff the lattice has maximal real rank \(2n\)

*** Covering space principle
- If \(\pi:M\to N\) is a covering map and \(N\) is complex, then \(M\) inherits a complex structure
- If deck transformations are holomorphic, the quotient inherits a complex structure

*** Hopf surface
- Quotient of \(\mathbf{C}^2 \setminus \{0\}\) by the automorphism \(z \mapsto 2z\)
- Example of a compact complex manifold not embeddable in projective space

---

** Tangent spaces on a complex manifold

*** Real tangent space
- \(T_{\mathbf{R},p}(M)\):
  - real tangent space of dimension \(2n\)

*** Complexified tangent space
- \(T_{\mathbf{C},p}(M) = T_{\mathbf{R},p}(M)\otimes_{\mathbf{R}} \mathbf{C}\)

*** Holomorphic tangent space
- \(T'_p(M)\):
  - spanned by \(\partial/\partial z_i\)

*** Antiholomorphic tangent space
- \(T''_p(M)\):
  - spanned by \(\partial/\partial \overline{z}_i\)

*** Splitting
- \(T_{\mathbf{C},p}(M) = T'_p(M) \oplus T''_p(M)\)

*** Intrinsic characterization
- \(T'_p(M)\):
  - derivations vanishing on antiholomorphic functions
- \(T''_p(M)\):
  - derivations vanishing on holomorphic functions

*** Conjugation
- Complex conjugation interchanges
  - \(T'_p(M)\)
  - \(T''_p(M)\)

*** Real-to-holomorphic identification
- Projection gives an \(\mathbf{R}\)-linear isomorphism
  - \(T_{\mathbf{R},p}(M) \to T'_p(M)\)

---

** Differential of a holomorphic map

*** Real differential
- A smooth map \(f:M\to N\) induces
  - \(f_*: T_{\mathbf{R},p}(M)\to T_{\mathbf{R},f(p)}(N)\)

*** Complexified differential
- Also induces
  - \(f_*: T_{\mathbf{C},p}(M)\to T_{\mathbf{C},f(p)}(N)\)

*** Holomorphicity criterion
- \(f\) is holomorphic iff
  - \(f_*(T'_p(M)) \subseteq T'_{f(p)}(N)\)
  for all \(p\)

---

** Jacobians and orientation

*** Real Jacobian
- In real coordinates, \(f_*\) is represented by a \(2m \times 2n\) real matrix

*** Complex Jacobian
- In holomorphic coordinates, use the matrix
  - \(\left(\partial f_i/\partial z_j\right)\)

*** Rank relation
- Real rank is twice the complex rank

*** Determinant relation
- When dimensions match:
  - \(\det J_{\mathbf{R}}(f) = |\det J_{\mathbf{C}}(f)|^2\)

*** Consequence
- Holomorphic maps preserve orientation

*** Natural orientation
- Complex manifolds have a canonical orientation induced from \(\mathbf{C}^n\)

---

** Inverse and implicit function theorems

*** Inverse Function Theorem
- If \(f:U\to V\) is holomorphic and
  - \(J_{\mathbf{C}}(f)\) is nonsingular at \(0\)
- Then:
  - \(f\) is locally biholomorphic
  - \(f^{-1}\) is holomorphic

*** Implicit Function Theorem
- Given holomorphic functions \(f_1,\dots,f_k\) with suitable nonsingular Jacobian minor
- Then locally:
  - the equations \(f_1=\cdots=f_k=0\) define holomorphic functions solving for \(k\) variables in terms of the remaining \(n-k\)

*** Special feature
- A one-to-one holomorphic map between open sets in \(\mathbf{C}^n\) automatically has nonsingular Jacobian everywhere
- Therefore its inverse is holomorphic

*** Contrast with real case
- This fails for smooth real maps

---

** Complex submanifolds and analytic subvarieties

*** Definition: complex submanifold
- A subset \(S \subset M\) is a complex submanifold if locally it is given either by:
  - zeros of holomorphic functions with maximal rank condition
- or by:
  - image of an open set in \(\mathbf{C}^{n-k}\) under a holomorphic map of rank \(n-k\)

*** Dimension
- Such an \(S\) is naturally a complex manifold of dimension \(n-k\)

*** Definition: analytic subvariety
- A subset locally defined by finitely many holomorphic equations

*** Smooth point
- \(p \in V\) is smooth if \(V\) is a submanifold near \(p\)

*** Singular locus
- \(V_s = V \setminus V^*\)

*** Smooth variety
- \(V\) is smooth iff all of its points are smooth

---

** Multiplicity of a hypersurface

*** Definition
- If \(V\) is a hypersurface defined locally by \(f\), then
  - \(\operatorname{mult}_p(V)\)
  is the order of vanishing of \(f\) at \(p\)

*** Characterization
- It is the largest integer \(m\) such that all partial derivatives of order \(< m\) vanish at \(p\)

** Generic terminology

*** Meaning of “generic”
- In a family parametrized by a complex manifold or analytic variety:
  - “generic” means
  - the exceptional set is contained in a subvariety of strictly smaller dimension

*** Note on projective language
- “Generic \(k\)-plane in \(\mathbf{P}^n\)” can be interpreted informally via the span of a generic \((k+1)\)-tuple of points

** Singular locus is small

*** Proposition
- \(V_s\) is contained in an analytic subvariety of \(M\) not equal to \(V\)

*** Meaning
- Singular points form a proper analytic subset

*** Proof mechanism
- Choose as many local defining equations as possible with a nontrivial Jacobian minor along \(V\)
- On the locus where that minor is nonzero:
  - \(V\) is a manifold
- Therefore singular points lie where the minors vanish

*** Stronger statement
- In fact \(V_s\) itself is analytic
- Not needed here

** Irreducibility versus connected smooth locus

*** Proposition
- \(V\) is irreducible iff \(V^*\) is connected

*** Easy direction
- If \(V = V_1 \cup V_2\), then \(V^*\) is disconnected

*** Harder direction
- Proven here only for hypersurfaces

*** Hypersurface proof idea
- Let \(V^*\) have connected components \(V_i\)
- Show each \(V_i\) extends to an analytic subvariety
- Use local Weierstrass form and symmetric functions of branches
- Build a holomorphic function vanishing exactly on \(V_i\)

** Dimension of an analytic variety

*** Irreducible case
